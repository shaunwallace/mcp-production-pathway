# Visual prompt — Three analogies for MCP

> Hero illustration for the Executive Brief. Output target: `fast-track/assets/exec-01-three-analogies.svg` (or `.png` if rendered raster).

## Concept

A clean three-panel triptych illustrating the analogies the brief uses to explain MCP to a non-technical audience: **shipping containers**, **power sockets in a hotel**, and **a printer-driver layer**. Each panel makes the same point — "before standardisation, every connection was bespoke; after, the connection is interchangeable" — in three different registers, so a reader can latch onto whichever lands for them.

The reader should grasp in under 8 seconds: *standardisation collapses bespoke wiring into interchangeable connectors.*

## Audience cue

Senior non-technical executive — CEO, CFO, COO, sales lead. Reading on a laptop or in a board pre-read PDF, possibly skimming. The triptych is doing the explanatory work that the prose elaborates; it must read at a glance, with no jargon, no technical icons.

## Required elements

**Three equal panels, side by side, each captioned underneath.**

### Panel 1 — Shipping containers

- Top half ("before"): a chaotic dockside scene rendered as flat geometric shapes — irregular bundles, sacks, crates of varying shapes, loose barrels, each with a bespoke shape. Tangled handling lines connecting them to a generic ship silhouette. Overall feel: noisy, asymmetric, fatigued.
- Bottom half ("after"): a tidy stack of identical rectangular shipping containers being lifted by a clean overhead crane onto the same ship. Uniform, calm, repeating module.
- Caption: **"Shipping containers — the cargo doesn't change. The interface does."**

### Panel 2 — Power sockets in a hotel

- Top half ("before"): a wall with five different appliances each plugged into a different bespoke socket shape (round, square, three-pin, two-pin, asymmetric). Tangled adapter cables visible.
- Bottom half ("after"): the same wall, but now five identical sockets, with the same five appliances plugged in cleanly via uniform plugs.
- Caption: **"A standard socket — the appliance and the wall stop caring about each other."**

### Panel 3 — A common driver layer

- Top half ("before"): three different operating-system silhouettes (rendered abstractly as labelled rounded rectangles: "OS A", "OS B", "OS C") each connected by a thicket of differently-styled lines to three different printer silhouettes. 9 bespoke connections, dense and mismatched.
- Bottom half ("after"): same OSes and printers, but a single horizontal "driver layer" band sits between them. Three clean lines from each OS into the band; three clean lines from the band to each printer.
- Caption: **"A driver standard — the OS doesn't need to know how each printer works."**

### Across the top, spanning all three panels:

- A unifying banner: **"Three shapes of the same idea: a standard interface."**
- Below the banner, a single small subtitle: *"MCP is this, for AI assistants and your business systems."*

## Style direction

- Clean, modern editorial-illustration register. Think *Stripe Press*, *The Economist* explainer graphics, *Vercel* marketing, *Anthropic* brand. Flat geometric, no gradients beyond very subtle elevation.
- Muted, professional palette: two or three colours plus neutral background. Suggested: a warm muted accent (terracotta or amber) for the "before" half of each panel, a cool accent (teal or slate blue) for the "after" half. Subtle, not garish.
- Generous whitespace between panels. Each panel should feel like its own contained vignette.
- Sans-serif typography (Inter, IBM Plex Sans, GT America, or similar). Captions should sit comfortably below each panel, not crowd the illustration.
- Soft drop shadows or subtle elevation on shipping containers / appliances are fine. Avoid heavy outlines.

## Aspect ratio / format

- 16:9 landscape (1920×1080), SVG preferred. Should also render cleanly at 1200px wide for inline use.
- Transparent background, or a single very pale neutral fill (not pure white, to avoid harshness in dark mode).

## Anti-requirements

- **No literal AI imagery** — no robots, no glowing brains, no neural-network meshes, no chatbot speech bubbles. The whole point is that the analogies are deliberately mundane.
- No 3D, no isometric perspective, no skeuomorphic photoreal renders. Flat editorial only.
- No human figures, no faces, no hands.
- No corporate logos (the printers and OSes are generic; do not draw Windows / macOS / specific printer brands).
- No flames, no warning signs, no "before = chaos / after = utopia" heavy-handedness. The density and uniformity contrast carries the message — let it.
- No icons sourced from generic icon libraries (Font Awesome, Material). The illustration should feel custom.

## Notes for the model / illustrator

The trickiest panel is **Panel 3 (driver layer)** — keep it as abstract as possible. Do not draw realistic computer monitors or printers; use very simple labelled rounded rectangles. The viewer should understand it as "systems talking to systems via a standard layer," not as a literal hardware diagram.

If the triptych feels visually overcrowded, reduce element counts (3 → 2 in any panel) before sacrificing whitespace.
